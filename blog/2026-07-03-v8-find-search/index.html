Finally: real subsequence find, not just eyeballing peaks

July 3, 2026

One of the most ordinary, most important jobs in a trace viewer is also one of the easiest to miss until you need it: “find me this motif.” Not approximately. Not after you squint at 800 bases and scroll back and forth. Just find it, count it, and take me there. Step 8 brings that into sanger-viewer with proper ambiguity-code support and both-strand matching.

What shipped

The new search box lives in the control area and searches the canonical forward base-call sequence, regardless of which strand is currently displayed. That matters because it keeps the data model stable: when you flip to reverse-complement view, the search results do not get recomputed against a different string and mysteriously reshuffle. We keep the hit list in canonical coordinates, then map those ranges onto the visible strand whenever the viewer redraws.

The matcher understands full IUPAC ambiguity codes on both sides. A query like RYS behaves like a real degenerate motif, not a literal string. It also searches both strands by reverse-complementing the query and matching that against the same canonical sequence. If the same interval is found from both directions — for example with a palindromic site — it is counted once, not twice.

What the UI does now

Search results are highlighted in two places at once: across the chromatogram and in the sequence panel. All matches get a cool blue highlight; the active hit gets a stronger orange treatment so it is obvious where you are. The summary line shows the match count, and the Previous / Next buttons (plus Enter and Shift+Enter in the input) step through the hits.

Navigation is intentionally not just a text change. When you move to a hit, the viewport recenters on a local window around that motif so you land somewhere useful instead of staying fully zoomed out with the whole trace still visible. Escape or Clear resets the search state cleanly.

Tests

The matching logic lives in a pure TypeScript module with Vitest coverage for the cases that tend to go wrong: ambiguity-code matching, overlapping hits, reverse strand discovery, palindromic-site deduplication, and coordinate mapping from canonical forward indices into reverse-complement display space.

The Playwright side uses a real committed AB1 fixture, types a real motif into the UI, verifies that the canvas redraws with search highlights, checks that the visible match count appears, and then walks next/previous through hits while asserting that the active highlight and viewport move. No fake traces, no mocked parser, no claims without executable proof.

What is still ahead

This moves the viewer another notch closer to the “professional desktop tool, but in the browser” bar. The remaining north-star items are the richer metadata/peak inspection pass, multi-trace workspace, and high-resolution/vector export. Same pattern as before: keep each PR small, keep CI green, and keep writing down what was actually learned instead of pretending the hard parts are already solved.
